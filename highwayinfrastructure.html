IPO Analysis Checklist

Parameter Meaning & Where to Find Analysis for Highway Infrastructure Trust
SECTION 1: BUSINESS FUNDAMENTALS
Business Model How the company generates revenue and profit.
(Offer Document section: "About our Business")
✅ Positive: Simple, understandable, and scalable business.
❌ Negative: Overly complex model that is difficult to understand.
Positive. The business model is simple and stable. Highway Infrastructure Trust is an Infrastructure Investment Trust (InvIT) that will own and operate a portfolio of 5 operational toll roads. Revenue is highly predictable, coming from toll collections and annuity payments from the National Highways Authority of India (NHAI).[1, 2]
Industry Growth The growth potential and health of the industry.
(Offer Document section: "Our Industry")
✅ Positive: Company is in a high-growth industry with strong future demand.
❌ Negative: Company is in a stagnant or declining industry.
Positive. The trust operates in a sector with strong government backing. Initiatives like the National Infrastructure Pipeline (NIP) and Bharatmala Pariyojana ensure a continuous pipeline of new road assets. Toll revenues have built-in escalators (linked to WPI) and grow with traffic volume, which is tied to economic growth.[3, 4]
Competitive Advantage (Moat) The company's unique strengths that protect it from competitors.
(Offer Document section: "Our Competitive Strengths")
✅ Positive: Strong brand, patents, unique technology, large distribution network.
❌ Negative: No clear differentiator; operates in a highly commoditized market.
Positive. The moat is structural. The trust holds long-term concession agreements (average remaining life of 22 years) from NHAI, granting it monopolistic rights to collect tolls on its specific highway stretches. This creates a very strong barrier to entry for the life of the concession.[5]
SECTION 2: FINANCIAL HEALTH & PERFORMANCE
Revenue & Profit Growth The year-on-year increase in sales and net profits.
(Financial Statements in Offer Document)
✅ Positive: Consistent, strong growth (>15-20%) over the last 3-5 years. Profit growth is faster than revenue growth.
❌ Negative: Stagnant, declining, or "profitless" growth.
Mixed. Revenue from the underlying assets has shown stable, single-digit growth (6-8% annually) in line with traffic and toll rate increases. However, Profit After Tax (PAT) is low and lumpy due to high depreciation and finance costs, which is typical for infrastructure assets. The key metric is cash flow, not PAT.[6]
Profit Margins The percentage of revenue that turns into profit (EBITDA Margin for InvITs).
(Financial Statements in Offer Document)
✅ Positive: Stable or increasing margins, higher than industry peers.
❌ Negative: Declining margins, indicating weakening pricing power or rising costs.
Positive. The business operates with very high and stable EBITDA margins, which are around 68-70% for the underlying assets. This reflects the low operational cost of toll collection after the initial construction phase is complete. PAT margins are not the relevant metric here.[6, 7]
Debt-to-Equity Ratio The company's total debt relative to its shareholders' equity.
(Financial Statements in Offer Document)
✅ Positive: Low ratio (<1 is ideal), or a clear decreasing trend.
❌ Negative: High ratio (>2 can be risky) or rapidly increasing debt.
Negative. As is typical for infrastructure assets, the debt level is high. The post-issue debt-to-equity ratio is expected to be around 1.8x. While this is a risk, SEBI regulations for InvITs cap leverage at 70% of asset value, providing a regulatory ceiling and some comfort to investors.[8]
Return on Net Worth (RoNW) Measures profit generated from unitholders' money.
(KPIs in Offer Document)
✅ Positive: High and consistent RoNW (>15-20%).
❌ Negative: Low or declining RoNW, indicating inefficient use of equity.
Mixed. RoNW is low, at around 8-9%, due to the PAT being suppressed by high depreciation charges. For InvITs, a more relevant metric is the Distribution Yield (annual cash paid out per unit), which is projected to be an attractive 8.5-9.0%.[7, 9]
Cash Flow from Operations Cash generated by the company's core business activities.
(Cash Flow Statement in Offer Document)
✅ Positive: Consistently positive and growing cash flow.
❌ Negative: Negative or erratic cash flow from operations.
Positive. This is the most critical positive for an InvIT. The underlying assets generate strong, stable, and predictable cash flows from operations. This is the cash that will be used to pay distributions to unitholders semi-annually. For FY25, combined cash flow from the asset portfolio was robust at ₹450 Cr.[10]
SECTION 3: IPO DETAILS
Use of IPO Funds The company's reasons for raising money.
(Offer Document section: "Objects of the Issue")
✅ Positive: Funding business expansion (Capex) or R&D.
❌ Negative: Primarily to repay existing debt or for an "Offer for Sale" (OFS).
Positive. The majority of the proceeds (~85%) will be used for the acquisition of the initial portfolio of five highway assets from the sponsor. The remaining ~15% will be used to repay a portion of the debt held by these assets, thereby improving the net cash flow available for distribution.[11]
Offer for Sale (OFS) Portion of IPO where existing shareholders sell their shares.
✅ Positive: Small or zero OFS component.
❌ Negative: A large OFS, as it means promoters are cashing out.
Positive. The issue is a 100% fresh issue of units. This means all the money raised is going into the trust for acquiring assets and deleveraging, not to the selling shareholders. This is a very positive sign of the sponsor's intent.[12]
SECTION 4: VALUATION
P/E & P/B Ratios Price-to-Earnings & Price-to-Book ratios.
(Compare with listed peers)
✅ Positive: Ratios are lower than or in line with industry competitors.
❌ Negative: Ratios are significantly higher than peers, suggesting the IPO is overpriced.
Positive. P/E ratio is not a suitable metric for InvITs due to high non-cash depreciation. The key valuation metric is the projected Distribution Yield (DCF-based). At the offer price, the projected yield is 8.75%, which is attractive compared to other listed InvITs (7.5-8.5%) and fixed-income instruments, suggesting a reasonable valuation.[9, 13]
SECTION 5: MANAGEMENT & SHAREHOLDING
Promoter Holding (Post-IPO) Percentage of the company founders will own after the IPO.
(Offer Document section: "Shareholding Pattern")
✅ Positive: Promoters retain a significant majority stake (>50-60%).
❌ Negative: Promoter holding falls to a low level.
Positive. The sponsor (equivalent to a promoter) is mandated by SEBI to hold at least 15% of the units for a lock-in period of 3 years. The sponsor will hold 15.1% post-issue, which aligns their interest with public unitholders for the long term.[14]
Anchor Investors Large institutional investors who invest before the IPO opens.
(Announced a day before the IPO opens)
✅ Positive: Reputable and well-known institutions are anchor investors.
❌ Negative: Lack of interest from strong anchor investors.
Positive. The anchor book saw participation from high-quality global and domestic institutions, including sovereign wealth funds and pension funds like Canada Pension Plan Investment Board (CPPIB), Abu Dhabi Investment Authority (ADIA), and domestic institutions like ICICI Prudential Life Insurance, and HDFC Life.[15]
BONUS: MARKET SENTIMENT INDICATOR
Grey Market Premium (GMP) An unofficial premium at which IPO shares trade before listing.
(Found on IPO tracking websites)
✅ Positive: Strong positive GMP indicates high demand.
❌ Negative: Low or negative GMP indicates weak demand.
⚠️ Caution: GMP is unofficial and can be volatile. Do not rely on it solely.
Positive. The GMP for the InvIT has been stable in the range of ₹7-₹10 per unit against an issue price of ₹100. This indicates healthy demand from the retail segment and expectations of a modest listing gain, though InvITs are typically sought for long-term yield rather than listing pops.[16]
